## I. The Hook: The Romanticized Lie

You've seen it. Everybody has. The chef in the spotless whites. Slow motion. One perfect garnish. The crowd goes quiet. Cut to commercial.

Bullshit.

The real thing? You're not just a chef. You're an accountant. A plumber. A therapist. You're fixing the POS at 7pm on a Saturday. You're running payroll at 2am. You're designing dishes that look good on a phone screen. You're cooking for people who eat with their eyes first and their stomachs never. The dream was food. The reality is business. And the business will break you if you're not ready.

---

## II. Running the Business (The Part Nobody Dreams About)

### You're Not Just Cooking

You wanted to cook. Good. Now you're also doing the books. Chasing vendors. Arguing with the landlord. Filling out permit renewals. Playing HR when someone no-shows. Playing IT when the printer dies. Playing diplomat when a table wants their meal comped because "it wasn't like the Instagram." You're a chef. You're also everything else. The restaurant doesn't care what your title says. It needs to run.

### The Spreadsheet Reality

Rent. Labor. Food cost. Utilities. Insurance. The health department. The grease trap. The AC. One number creeps up and the whole thing tilts. You're not making food anymore—you're moving decimals. You're cutting hours. You're switching purveyors. You're hoping Tuesday covers Wednesday. The margins are thin. The spreadsheets don't lie. Neither does the bank.

### The Operator's Trap

You opened a restaurant to cook. You're running a business that happens to serve food. That distinction will haunt you. The creative part—the part you love—gets squeezed into the cracks. The rest is logistics. Hiring. Firing. Scheduling. Inventory. Compliance. It's not glamorous. It's not on TV. It's most of the job.

---

## III. Instagram Food: Cooking for the Camera

### Picture First, Taste Second

The table sits down. The phone comes out. "Can we get a photo?" They're not ordering yet—they're staging. The dish has to travel. It has to sit under heat lamps while they get the angle right. The cheese pull. The steam. The drip. By the time they eat it, it's dead. They don't care. They got the shot.

You're not cooking for mouths anymore. You're cooking for screens. And the screen doesn't taste anything.

### The Viral Dish Problem

Every restaurant needs "that dish." The one people post. The one that goes on the menu because it photographs well, not because it eats well. So you build it. Over the top. Garnishes on garnishes. Colors that pop. Architecture that collapses the second a fork touches it. It sells. It gets shared. It looks nothing like what arrives at the table—because what arrives has been sitting under a heat lamp for six minutes while someone found the right filter. You know it's wrong. You do it anyway. The algorithm demands it.

### Yelp, Influencers, and the Algorithm

Someone with 40K followers walks in. They get comped. They post. You hope it's good. Sometimes it isn't. The review goes up anyway. One star. "Wasn't worth the hype." You fed them for free. They fed you to the algorithm. The algorithm doesn't care about your food. It cares about engagement. And nothing engages like drama. You're not just running a kitchen. You're running a content farm. With knives.

---

## IV. The Old Problems (Still There)

### The Body, The Mind, The Sacrifice

None of this replaced the old stuff. Your knees still hurt. Your back still goes out. You're still missing holidays. You're still lying awake at 2am replaying the refire. The physical toll. The mental toll. The wage gap. The substance abuse. The camaraderie that makes it all bearable—and the culture that makes it toxic. We've covered that before. It hasn't changed. It's just that now you've got new problems on top of it. Running the business. Feeding the feed.

### The Line Stays Real

Through all of it—the spreadsheets, the Instagram, the landlord, the POS—the line keeps moving. Tickets keep coming. People keep eating. You keep cooking. Or trying to. The craft is still there. Buried under the business. Under the performance. Under the demand that every plate be a photo op. You dig for it. Some nights you find it. Some nights you don't. The line doesn't care. It just keeps going.

---

## V. Conclusion: What It Actually Takes Now

### The Honest Truth

Running a restaurant in 2025 means cooking and business and content strategy all at once. You wanted to make good food. Fine. You'll also make good spreadsheets. Good Instagram. Good decisions at 11pm when the walk-in goes down. The romanticized version is dead. The real version is harder. It's also the only one that survives.

### A Note for the Rest of You

**If you eat in restaurants:** Eat the food. Put the phone down. Tip like you mean it. The person behind the pass is human. The dish was made for you, not for your feed.

**If you run a restaurant:** Plan. Know your numbers. Pay people. And maybe—maybe—stop designing every dish for the camera. Some food is meant to be eaten, not posted. That's okay.
